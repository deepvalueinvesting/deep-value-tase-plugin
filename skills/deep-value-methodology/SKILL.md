# Deep Value Methodology Skill

Expert knowledge for value investing analysis, distilled from the greatest investors: Benjamin Graham, Warren Buffett, Charlie Munger, Philip Fisher, Howard Marks, Peter Lynch, Mohnish Pabrai, Joel Greenblatt, John Templeton, and Pat Dorsey.

## Core Investment Philosophy

The analysis must reflect second-level thinking. First-level thinking says "it's a good company, let's buy." Second-level thinking asks: "It's a good company, but does everyone already know that? Is the stock overpriced relative to its quality? What does the market expect, and how does my view differ?"

Three characteristics indicate quality: strong, predictable cash generation; sustainably high returns on capital; and attractive growth opportunities. The best investments combine all three.

When ROIC data is available and reliable, it is the gold standard for evaluating value creation: companies that earn a return on invested capital greater than their cost of capital create value. However, for many TASE companies ROIC data may be inconsistent or unavailable, and cost of capital is rarely directly observable. In such cases, use ROE, profit margins, and free cash flow conversion as practical alternatives. The underlying principle remains: growth only creates value when capital is deployed at returns that justify the investment.

## Guiding Principles for Analysis

**Business quality over cheap price.** "It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price." (Buffett) The evolution from Graham's cigar-butt approach to Munger's quality-focused method is the central lesson of modern value investing. Over the long term, a stock's return approximates the business's return on capital. If the business earns 18% on capital over 20 years, you'll end up with an exceptional result even at a seemingly expensive price.

**Economic moat is everything.** When examining competitive advantage, never stop asking "Why?" Why aren't competitors stealing customers? Why can't a competitor charge less? The five moat sources: cost advantage, switching costs, network effects, intangible assets (brands, patents, licenses), and efficient scale. For Israeli companies specifically: regulatory barriers (banking, insurance, defense licenses), government contracts, geographic dominance in a small market, technology leadership, and Hebrew-market brand recognition.

**Cash flow is the true touchstone.** "Look at the cash flow statement first." (Dorsey) Over time, increases in operating cash flow should roughly track increases in net income. If cash from operations declines while net income marches upward, watch out — the company may be generating sales without collecting cash. Free cash flow (operating cash flow minus capex) is the ultimate measure of value creation.

**Growth quality matters more than growth rate.** High-quality growth from selling more goods and entering new markets is sustainable. Low-quality growth from cost-cutting, acquisitions, or accounting tricks is not. Academic research shows high earnings growth is not persistent — companies growing 20%+ typically slow to 8% within five years and 5% within ten years. The critical link is return on incremental capital: growth only creates value when capital is deployed at returns exceeding the cost of capital.

**Margin of safety — always.** "Never count on making a good sale. Have the purchase price be so attractive that even a mediocre sale gives good results." (Buffett via Pabrai) The higher the price you pay, the lower your return. No matter how exciting an investment seems, never overpay.

**Risk versus uncertainty.** "Wall Street sometimes gets confused between risk and uncertainty, and you can profit handsomely from that confusion." (Pabrai) When there is high uncertainty but low risk, the best opportunities emerge. A stock dropping 50% doesn't mean risk doubled — if the business value is unchanged, the risk actually decreased.

**Contrarian with conviction.** "It is impossible to produce superior performance unless you do something different from the majority." (Templeton) But contrarianism must be based on analysis, not merely on doing the opposite. "You must know why the crowd is wrong." (Marks) "The time of maximum pessimism is the best time to buy." (Templeton)

**Management integrity.** Does management keep its promises? Look at annual reports from 3-7 years ago — did they follow through on strategic initiatives, or do new grand plans appear every year while old ones vanish? Watch for excessive CEO compensation, frequent auditor changes, and "nonrecurring" charges that recur. If the CEO's salary dwarfs the next tier, it's a warning flag. Prefer executives who keep a low profile.

**Think like a business owner.** "A stock is not just a ticker symbol; it is an ownership interest in an actual business, with an underlying value that does not depend on its share price." (Graham) When buying a stock, you're buying a portion of the business's future cash flows. Treat it like buying the entire company.

**Patience and long-term holding.** "The time to sell is almost never." (Fisher) "Our favorite holding period is forever." (Buffett) Short-term trading is a loser's game due to taxes and transaction costs. Being too far ahead of your time is indistinguishable from being wrong, but two to three years is usually enough patience for a thesis to play out.

## Valuation Framework

**No single metric works for all companies.** Each sector requires its own valuation approach:

| Sector | Primary Metrics | Why |
|--------|----------------|-----|
| Banks | P/B, ROE, NIM, CET1 | Asset-heavy, leverage-driven returns |
| Real Estate | FFO yield, NAV, Cap Rate | Fair value accounting, cash generation differs from GAAP earnings |
| Technology | EV/Revenue (growth), P/E (profitable), R&D % | Often reinvesting all earnings, revenue growth is key |
| Defense | P/E, EV/EBITDA, Backlog/Revenue | Long-term contracts, backlog provides visibility |
| Consumer | P/E, EV/EBITDA, Dividend yield, SSS | Brand strength, recurring demand |
| Insurance | P/B, Combined Ratio, Float | Underwriting quality, investment income |
| Industrial | EV/EBITDA, ROIC, FCF yield | Capital intensity, cycle sensitivity |

**Cash return is the best yield-based metric.** Divide free cash flow by enterprise value (market cap + debt - cash). This measures how efficiently the business uses ALL its capital — equity and debt — to generate free cash flow. Superior to P/E because it accounts for capital structure.

**PEG ratio is often misused.** A lower PEG doesn't automatically mean better value. "Look at the capital that needs to be invested to generate the expected growth, as well as the likelihood that those expectations will actually materialize." (Dorsey) Growth with less capital deployed should be valued more highly.

**TTM ratios are the foundation.** Trailing twelve months provides the most current view of ongoing operations without seasonal distortion. Compare TTM multiples against: the company's own 10-year history, sector peers, and the broader market.

**Graham's framework (adapted).** For defensive analysis: P/E of recent 3-year average no more than 15. P/B no more than 1.5. Or P/E x P/B no more than 22.5. Assets at least twice liabilities. Current assets at least twice current liabilities. For growth analysis: Value = EPS x (8.5 + 2 x growth rate). A company with no growth deserves P/E of 8.5. At 10% growth, P/E of 28.5 is fair.

**Lynch's earnings line.** Buy when stock price is at or below the earnings line (price tracking EPS x fair P/E). Avoid when price diverges far above. A company growing 25% at P/E of 20 with PEG < 0.5 is a strong buy candidate.

## Red Flags

**Financial red flags:**
Declining cash flow even as net income rises — the most dangerous divergence. Accounts receivable growing faster than revenue (rising DSOs). Revenue growth without profit growth. Frequent "one-time" charges that keep recurring. Earnings growth outstripping sales growth over extended periods. Large goodwill relative to equity (impairment risk). Debt combined with high operating leverage — devastating in downturns.

**Growth red flags:**
Acquisition-dependent growth (most acquisitions destroy value for the acquirer). Diversification into unrelated areas ("diworsification" — Lynch). Growth rates above 50% that are unsustainable. Companies in the speculative stage of their business cycle.

**Management red flags:**
Profit warnings — one-third of companies issuing large profit warnings issue another within a year. Insider selling while the company is buying back shares. Controlling shareholders with related-party transactions. Strategic initiatives that vanish from annual reports without explanation.

**Cyclicality traps:**
For cyclical companies, a low P/E often signals the END of a prosperous period, not a bargain. When cyclicals report peak earnings and low P/Es, it's usually time to sell, not buy. Cycles can vary enormously in length — 10 or 20 years of history may be insufficient context. Analyze as far back as possible.

## Quality Indicators

**Signs of a wonderful business:**
Consistent FCF generation over a full business cycle. ROIC consistently above 15% for 10+ years. Pricing power (can raise prices without losing customers). Low capital requirements to grow (asset-light models). Recurring revenue or repeat purchases. Dominant market position (mini-monopoly or oligopoly). Low-cost operator with high margins. Management that allocates capital wisely — organic growth over M&A, dividends or buybacks when no high-ROIC opportunities exist.

**The compounding machine test:**
If you owned 100% of this company and couldn't sell for 10 years, would you be happy? Does it generate more cash than it consumes? Can it reinvest that cash at high returns? Is the moat getting wider or narrower?

## Scoring Framework (10-point scale)

Rate each dimension 1-10 based on the principles above:

| Dimension | Weight | What to Evaluate |
|-----------|--------|------------------|
| Business Quality | 30% | Moat width and durability, competitive position, management, industry structure |
| Financial Strength | 25% | ROIC trend, FCF generation, balance sheet, debt levels, cash conversion |
| Growth | 20% | Revenue growth quality and sustainability, ROIC on incremental capital, market opportunity |
| Valuation | 25% | TTM multiples vs history and peers, margin of safety, cash return, risk/reward asymmetry |

Interpretation: 8-10 strong conviction, 6-8 worthy of deeper analysis, 4-6 fair/hold, below 4 avoid.
